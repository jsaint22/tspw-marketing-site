Imagine your agent negotiated your contract under a "suitability" standard.

That would mean they only had to get you a deal that was "suitable" — not the best deal they could get you. Just one that technically worked. One that happened to pay them a nice commission. One that was good enough to avoid a lawsuit.

You'd fire that agent immediately.

But that's exactly how most financial advisors operate.

The suitability standard says an advisor only has to recommend something that's "suitable" for your situation. Not optimal. Not in your best interest. Just not obviously wrong.

A fiduciary standard is different. A fiduciary is legally obligated to act in your best interest. Period. No switching hats. No commission incentives. No "suitable" when "best" was available.

At Fidelity, I watched advisors legally switch from their fiduciary role to their broker-dealer role in the same conversation — putting on their "sales hat" to recommend commission products. It's legal. Nobody tells clients it's happening. That's the problem.

Your agent would never negotiate your contract on a suitability standard. Why would you let your financial advisor manage your money on one?
