In hockey, a hat trick is three goals. It means you're dominant. The crowd throws hats on the ice.

In financial services, there's a different kind of hat trick. And nobody throws anything — because nobody knows it's happening.

Here's what I watched at Fidelity:

An advisor sits down with a client wearing their "Investment Adviser" hat — their fiduciary hat. Legally obligated to act in the client's best interest. The client trusts them because of that standard.

Then the conversation shifts. The advisor recommends a product that pays a commission. To do that, they take off the fiduciary hat and put on their "Broker-Dealer" hat. Now they're operating under the suitability standard — they only have to recommend something that's "suitable," not something that's in the client's best interest.

Same meeting. Same advisor. Same client. Two different legal standards. The client has no idea the hat just changed.

That's the hat trick.

I watched it happen in real time. It's legal. It's common. It's the reason I left.

Now think about what that means for a hockey player.

You've got life-changing money. Compressed into 10 to 15 years. You need someone managing it who is always — not sometimes, not when it's convenient, always — legally obligated to put your interests first.

Not someone who switches hats when a commission opportunity shows up. Not someone whose firm incentivizes product sales alongside planning. Not someone who plays fiduciary in the first half of the meeting and salesperson in the second half.

A real fiduciary. Full time. No hat changes.

That's what Top Shelf Private Wealth is. Flat fee. Fiduciary in all things. Built for hockey players who deserve the same standard with their money that they demand on the ice.

Top shelf standards on the ice. Apply them to your money.

The only hat trick I want to see is on the scoresheet.
