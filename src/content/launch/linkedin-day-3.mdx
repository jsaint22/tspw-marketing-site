You play 82 games a year. Some in California. Some in Florida. Some in New York. Some in Canada.

Every single one of those games creates a tax obligation in a different jurisdiction.

It's called the jock tax. And it is the most expensive thing nobody talks to hockey players about.

Here's what it actually looks like:

You play a road game in New York. New York taxes the income you earned that day at their state rate — up to 10.9%. Then your home state may or may not give you a credit for what you paid New York.

Multiply that by every road game. Every state and province. Every tax return.

A player earning $3M per year can easily owe taxes in 15 to 20 jurisdictions. If nobody's coordinating that, you're overpaying. Not by a little. By tens of thousands of dollars every season.

And most advisors? They don't even know what the jock tax is. They hand you off to a generalist CPA who files your home state return and calls it done.
